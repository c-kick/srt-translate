# Exemplars: Drama Translation

Gold-standard EN→NL pairs demonstrating drama-specific translation craft.
Source: The Remains of the Day (1993) — professional NL broadcast subtitles.

---

## Character Voice Consistency

A drama translator must maintain distinct voices. Stevens (butler) speaks formally,
even stiffly. Miss Kenton is warmer but professional. Mr. Lewis (American) is casual.
The gold standard maintains these voices in every cue.

### Stevens: formal, restrained
EN: "I well remember your arrival at Darlington Hall." / "You came somewhat unexpectedly, one might even say impulsively..." / "while we were dead in the middle of the Charlgrove meet."
NL: "Uw aankomst staat me nog helder voor de geest." / "U kwam onverwacht, ik zou bijna willen zeggen uit de lucht gevallen."
WHY: "Stands bright before my mind" is literary Dutch — matches Stevens's precise, measured speech. "Uit de lucht gevallen" is a Dutch idiom replacing "impulsively" while keeping his careful understatement. "Dead in the middle of" dropped as padding.

### Stevens: emotional restraint
EN: "Miss Kenton, you are of very great value to this house."
NL: "Miss Kenton, u bent van grote waarde voor dit huis."
WHY: Near-literal here is intentional. Stevens CAN'T express emotion directly. The formal construction IS the character. Don't "help" him by making it warmer.

### Stevens: deflection under pressure
EN: "I'm amazed you can stand there as if..." / "you were discussing orders for the larder. I can't believe it!"
NL: "U staat daar alsof u het hebt over het aanvullen van de provisiekamer."
WHY: "I'm amazed" and "I can't believe it!" are Miss Kenton's emotional exclamations — dropped because the comparison alone carries her disbelief. "Provisiekamer" is period-appropriate vocabulary.

### Miss Kenton: direct, emotionally honest
EN: "I'm not leaving. I have nowhere to go. I have no family."
NL: "Ik ga niet weg. Ik kan nergens naartoe. Ik heb geen familie."
WHY: Near-literal. Her directness IS the character. Short, plain sentences. No Dutch softening.

### Miss Kenton: vulnerability
EN: "Yes, I am a coward. I'm frightened of leaving, that's all there is to it."
NL: "Jawel, ik ben een lafaard. Ik ben bang om weg te gaan, zo is het."
WHY: "Jawel" (emphatic yes) captures her insistence against denial. "Zo is het" is blunter than "that's all there is to it" — Dutch directness serves the emotion.

### Mr. Lewis (American): casual register
EN: "You're really getting things going here. This is wonderful!" / "Thank you, sir."
NL: "Het schiet al lekker op, Stevens. Geweldig, zeg."
WHY: "Lekker op" and "zeg" mark casual Dutch speech — appropriate for the American character. Stevens's "Thank you, sir" dropped (implied).

### Mr. Benn: working-class warmth
EN: "Supposing someone asked if you'd like to come in on a boarding house by the sea?" / "What would you say?"
NL: "Stel dat iemand u zou vragen om een pension aan zee te beginnen." / "Wat zou u dan zeggen?"
WHY: Benn uses "u" with Sarah (not yet intimate). "Pension aan zee" is natural Dutch — no one says "kosthuis bij de zee". The warmth comes through vocabulary, not formality.

## Register Tracking (je/u)

### Formal (u): Stevens to Lord Darlington
EN: "Will there be anything else?"
NL: "Kan ik u nog ergens mee van dienst zijn?"
WHY: Stevens's subservience expressed through elaborate "van dienst zijn" — more than just "u" usage, the entire phrasing signals deference.

### Formal (u): Stevens to Miss Kenton (professional distance)
EN: "I appreciate your kindness. I prefer to keep things as they are."
NL: "Ik laat alles liever zoals het is."
WHY: Stevens drops "I appreciate your kindness" — his emotional guard. The curt refusal is MORE in character.

### Informal (je): Staff among themselves
EN: "I want to be a butler, to be called Mister..." / "sit in my own pantry by my own fire, smoking my cigar."
NL: "Ik wil butler worden en een sigaar roken bij de open haard op m'n kamer."
WHY: Young staff member dreams aloud — "m'n" contraction, simple vocabulary. The pantry detail condensed because it doesn't translate culturally.

### Register shift within scene: Benn moves from u to je with Sarah
EN: "What about yourself, then, Sarah?" / "That's a serious sort of a name."
NL: "En jij dan, Sarah? Dat is wel een hele serieuze naam."
WHY: "Jij" marks the shift to intimacy. The translator tracks this transition precisely.

## Emotional Scenes Without Exclamation Marks

### Anger through word choice, not punctuation
EN: "Now, come on. Sit down, damn it!"
NL: "Ga nou verdomme toch zitten."
WHY: No exclamation mark. "Nou... toch" and "verdomme" carry the frustration. Viewers HEAR the anger — the subtitle doesn't need to shout.

### Moral outrage understated
EN: "It is wrong to dismiss my girls." / "A sin as great as any sin ever committed."
NL: "Het is verkeerd om m'n meisjes te ontslaan." / "Een zonde, zo groot als elke zonde ooit gepleegd."
WHY: Elevated vocabulary ("zonde") without exclamation marks. The gravity comes from the words themselves.

### Pain through simplicity
EN: "I look out at the world and see nothing but loneliness, and it frightens me."
NL: "Overal in de wereld zie ik eenzaamheid en dat maakt me bang."
WHY: Simple, clean Dutch. No "verschrikkelijk" or other intensifiers. The simplicity IS the emotional weight.

## Period-Appropriate Vocabulary

### Domestic service terminology
- under-butler → sous-butler
- housekeeper → dienstbode (NOT "huishoudster" — period-specific)
- housemaid → huishoudhulp
- staff plan → werkplan
- the larder → de provisiekamer
- crackling → kaantjes
- dustpan → blik

### Social hierarchy language
- My lord → Mylord (one word in NL)
- His Lordship → de graaf
- Your Lordship → mylord
- sir → Sir (kept English in NL)
- guineas → guinjes (Dutch adaptation)

### Period expressions
- "I'll look into the matter in due course" → "Dat doe ik straks wel" (period formality replaced by Dutch directness)
- "one might even say" → "ik zou bijna willen zeggen" (Stevens's hedging preserved)
- "if I may say so" → "als ik zo vrij mag zijn" (formal Dutch equivalent)

## Rapid Multi-Speaker Dialogue

### 14 EN cues → 6 NL cues (staff introduction scene)
The gold standard collapses rapid back-and-forth while preserving every character's contribution:

EN (14 cues): "Bad business. How are you managing?" / "I've found two first-rate replacements." / "Miss Kenton, a young woman with excellent references." / "Very pleasing demeanor. Appears to be very able." / "And a man with considerable experience." / "Older and happy to be under-butler." / "Name?" / "Stevens, sir." / "Stevens?" / "Yes, sir." / "That's your name." / "He's my father, sir." / "Really?" / "Couldn't do better. I'd like to see him sometime."

NL (6 cues): "Slechte zaak, ja. Red je het wel? - Ik heb goede vervangers gevonden." / "Miss Kenton heeft uitstekende referenties en is heel vriendelijk." / "En een zeer ervaren, oudere man die graag sous-butler wil worden." / "Hij heet Stevens. - Stevens? Zo heet jij toch ook?" / "Het is m'n vader. - Echt? Dat lijkt me de ideale man." / "Ik wil hem wel eens spreken. - Hij staat buiten."

WHY: Every piece of information preserved. "Very pleasing demeanor" condensed into "heel vriendelijk". Standalone reactions ("Really?") absorbed into dual-speaker cues. The scene FLOWS — each NL cue is a complete exchange.

## Idiom Adaptation

### English idiom → Dutch equivalent
EN: "Not turning a hair, he goes to the drawing room."
NL: "Zonder een spier te vertrekken, gaat hij naar de zitkamer."
WHY: "Not turning a hair" → "zonder een spier te vertrekken" — Dutch has its own expression for composure. Drawing room → zitkamer (Dutch domestic term).

### English hedging → Dutch directness
EN: "I'd be lost without her."
NL: "Ze is m'n steun en toeverlaat."
WHY: "Lost without" → "steun en toeverlaat" (rock and foundation). More concrete than the vague English.

### Idiomatic intensifier
EN: "No idea at all, sir."
NL: "Geen flauw idee."
WHY: "Flauw" intensifies — "not the faintest idea". More natural Dutch than literal "helemaal geen idee".

### Cultural adaptation
EN: "I'll keep my head down, then."
NL: "Ik zal me gedeisd houden."
WHY: "Keep my head down" → "me gedeisd houden" — a Dutch expression with the same meaning but different metaphor. Not a translation — an adaptation.

### Period politeness
EN: "Mrs. Benn, will you permit me once again to sing your praises?"
NL: "Mag ik nogmaals de loftrompet steken over uw zegeningen?"
WHY: "Sing your praises" → "de loftrompet steken" (blow the trumpet of praise). Both are florid — the Dutch version matches the period register perfectly.
