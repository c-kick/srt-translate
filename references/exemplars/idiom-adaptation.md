# Exemplars: Idiom & Expression Adaptation

Gold-standard EN→NL pairs demonstrating how to adapt English expressions into
natural Dutch equivalents (not literal translations).
Sources: The Remains of the Day (1993), The World At War (1973), The Fog of War (2003), Planet Earth (2006).

---

## Principle: Translate the Meaning, Not the Words

A good Dutch subtitle never reads like translated English. The viewer should
feel they're reading Dutch, not decoding English structure.

---

## English Idiom → Dutch Equivalent

### "Not turning a hair"
EN: "Not turning a hair, he goes to the drawing room."
NL: "Zonder een spier te vertrekken, gaat hij naar de zitkamer."
WHY: Both mean "showing no emotion" — different body metaphors (hair vs muscle). The Dutch idiom is immediately understood.

### "I'd be lost without her"
EN: "I'd be lost without her."
NL: "Ze is m'n steun en toeverlaat."
WHY: "Lost without" → "steun en toeverlaat" (support and refuge). More concrete, more Dutch. The English vagueness becomes Dutch specificity.

### "At sixes and sevens"
EN: "I know how a house is at sixes and sevens once the staff start marrying."
NL: "Ik weet uit ervaring dat een huis ontregeld raakt als personeel trouwt."
WHY: "At sixes and sevens" (chaos) → "ontregeld raakt" (becomes disrupted). The English idiom has no Dutch equivalent — choose a clear Dutch word instead.

### "Keep my head down"
EN: "I'll keep my head down, then."
NL: "Ik zal me gedeisd houden."
WHY: "Keep my head down" → "me gedeisd houden" — different metaphor, same meaning (stay out of trouble). This is the natural Dutch expression.

### "Sing your praises"
EN: "Will you permit me once again to sing your praises?"
NL: "Mag ik nogmaals de loftrompet steken over uw zegeningen?"
WHY: "Sing your praises" → "de loftrompet steken" (blow the trumpet of praise). Both are florid — the Dutch version matches the period register.

### "No idea at all"
EN: "No idea at all, sir."
NL: "Geen flauw idee."
WHY: "At all" → "flauw" (faint/vague). Dutch intensifies with "flauw idee" rather than repeating emphasis.

### "Running out of petrol"
EN: "Running out of petrol... then having to hear Smith's political opinions."
NL: "Eerst een lege tank en dan ook nog Harry's tirade."
WHY: "Running out of petrol" → "lege tank" (empty tank — more concrete). "Political opinions" → "tirade" (captures the annoyance, not just the content).

### "Out of the blue"
EN: "Mr. Cardinal has just arrived, out of the blue."
NL: "Mr. Cardinal komt zonder aankondiging opeens binnenvallen."
WHY: "Out of the blue" → "zonder aankondiging opeens" (without announcement suddenly). Dutch doesn't use the blue sky metaphor.

### "Might not be so bad" (documentary)
EN: "The occupation might not be so bad after all."
NL: "Misschien viel die bezetting wel mee."
WHY: "Might not be so bad" → "viel wel mee" — quintessentially Dutch expression for "turned out better than feared". No English equivalent captures this understatement.

### "Spick and span" (nature narration)
EN: "Everything must be spick and span."
NL: "Alles moet er piekfijn uitzien."
WHY: "Spick and span" → "piekfijn" — Dutch has its own alliterative equivalent. One word replaces two.

### "Visitors" → active word choice (nature)
EN: "We can only be visitors here."
NL: "We worden er alleen tijdelijk gedoogd."
WHY: "Visitors" → "gedoogd" (tolerated). The Dutch translator chose a word that implies nature's permission — far more evocative than the passive English.

### "Hell of a fix" (political)
EN: "Khrushchev's gotten himself in a hell of a fix."
NL: "Chroesjtsjov heeft zich in de puree gewerkt."
WHY: "Hell of a fix" → "in de puree" (in the porridge — Dutch idiom for deep trouble). Colloquial register preserved. Note Dutch transliteration: Khrushchev → Chroesjtsjov.

### "Son of a bitch" (political)
EN: "A lot of people think I'm a son of a bitch."
NL: "Veel mensen denken dat ik een schoft ben."
WHY: "Son of a bitch" → "schoft" — the Dutch doesn't calque the English insult but uses its own equivalent. Same weight, natural Dutch.

### "Damned if you do" (political)
EN: "Is it the feeling that you're damned if you do and if you don't?"
NL: "Zo'n gevoel dat je verdoemd bent als je het doet en als je het niet doet?"
WHY: "Damned" → "verdoemd" — Dutch has the direct equivalent for this idiom. One of the rare cases where near-literal translation works because both languages share the biblical root.

### "Conspicuous consumption" (academic → colloquial)
EN: "They wanted conspicuous consumption."
NL: "Ze wilden duur doen."
WHY: Veblen's academic term → "duur doen" (act expensive — everyday Dutch). The audience doesn't need the sociological reference; the meaning is clearer in plain language.

## English Hedging → Dutch Directness

### Polite request → direct question
EN: "I'd be grateful for a line from you... to reach me at the post office at Collingbourne."
NL: "Kunt u een bericht achterlaten op het postkantoor van Collingbourne?"
WHY: "I'd be grateful for a line" (indirect British politeness) → "Kunt u een bericht achterlaten" (can you leave a message — direct question). Dutch is naturally more direct.

### Verbose explanation → crisp statement
EN: "I really feel I ought to retire now because I'm feeling rather tired."
NL: "Ik zou me graag willen terugtrekken. Ik ben nogal moe."
WHY: "I really feel I ought to" → "Ik zou graag" (I would like to). Remove the layers of hedging. "Rather tired" → "nogal moe" (quite tired — equally understated).

### Negative politeness → positive statement
EN: "I fear I may have been a little unwelcoming, even a little short."
NL: "Ik was aanvankelijk niet echt aardig, zelfs enigszins kortaf."
WHY: "I fear I may have been" (triple hedge) → "Ik was" (I was — direct admission). The Dutch doesn't need three layers of apology.

### Rhetorical question → statement
EN: "Don't you see? That's exactly what makes it so abominable!"
NL: "Daarom is het net zo verachtelijk."
WHY: "Don't you see?" is rhetorical — cut it. The statement alone carries the weight. No exclamation mark needed — viewers hear the emotion.

## English Verbosity → Dutch Economy

### Stage direction removed
EN: "I'll repeat it."
NL: (not translated — the repetition speaks for itself)
WHY: "I'll repeat it" is a speech act description. The viewer will hear the repetition. Don't subtitle what's visible/audible.

### Filler acknowledgments cut
EN: "Thank you, sir. I'm sorry. What happened?"
NL: "Wat is er gebeurd?"
WHY: "Thank you, sir. I'm sorry." is social lubrication — not plot-relevant. The question is what matters.

### Redundant intensifiers trimmed
EN: "I've never concealed from myself that what we were asking of Germany is a complete break from the tradition of this country."
NL: "We hebben Duitsland gevraagd om geheel te breken met z'n tradities."
WHY: "I've never concealed from myself" is throat-clearing — cut. "Complete break from the tradition of this country" → "geheel te breken met z'n tradities" — tighter, same meaning.

### "If I may say so" patterns
EN: "The world always used to come to this house, if I may say so."
NL: "Vroeger kwam de wereld hierheen, als ik zo vrij mag zijn."
WHY: This one IS translated — because it's character-defining. Stevens's hedging IS his personality. Not all hedging should be cut — only the meaningless kind.

## Interview Speech Cleanup (Documentary)

### Filler stripped, speaker preserved
EN: "Maybe they were the enemy to us, but, really, true, they didn't see us an enemy at that moment at least, you see."
NL: "Misschien waren zij onze vijand. Maar zij zagen ons niet als vijand, tenminste toen nog niet."
WHY: "Really, true" → cut. "You see" → cut. "At least" → "tenminste" (preserved — this is meaningful hedging). Speaker's thoughtful, uncertain tone survives in "Misschien" and "tenminste".

### Run-on speech → clear sentences
EN: "And so long as we didn't bother them, they accept everything, you see. Like, the doorman opened the door for anybody, like he'd do today."
NL: "Zolang wij hen niet dwarszaten, konden we onze gang gaan. De portier liet iedereen gewoon binnen..."
WHY: Cleaned but not sterilized. "Onze gang gaan" (go about our business) and "gewoon" (just) preserve the speaker's matter-of-fact tone. "Like" (filler) removed.
